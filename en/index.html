Essential information to live freely in a decentralized society, version 1


  1. Starting point: human rights

    The United Nations’ Universal Declaration of Human Rights is outdated and disputed (see Žižek's dissertation on the topic), but its 30 articles constitute a good starting point to define the rights that every human should enjoy.

  2. What we mean by decentralization

    We take decentralization to mean not the delegation of government from a central one to smaller subordinate governments, but the transformation of services and institutions to a form where there is no central controlling entity; rather control emerges from the collective will of the people, like a colony of ants shows intelligent behavior without there being a central authority, only a simple set of rules shared by every member of society.

  3. What we would like to change

    Most of us currently live in centralized nation states, pay taxes on their earnings, vote in elections at various levels, use a national or supranational currency, use their national ID to prove their identity, and deal with slow and inconvenient institutions and their bureaucracy every day. The advances made in the past 30 years (namely: the Internet, widespread use of powerful personal computers, and cryptocurrencies) represent powerful tools for the gradual emancipation of the people from the increasingly superfluous nation states we live in. Decentralized systems are preferable wherever they are possible, as they are largely immune to many of the flaws of centralized institutions (corruption, waste, delays, high cost, bureaucracy, censorship...). This concept has been explored by many academics; here an excerpt from Leopold Kohr's The Breakdown of Nations:

    "...why should not as many individuals have as many different institutions as they like instead of having all to use a single costume which half of them might consider not to their taste? If freedom of choice is considered an advantage economically, why not also politically? For, with a great multitude of systems prevailing in an area inhabited by hundreds of millions of people, it becomes mathematically inevitable that far more individuals are able to obtain what their hearts desire than if the same region were to permit only a single system, even as in a restaurant many more people can obtain satisfaction if the menu includes a great variety of dishes rather than a single one which can be made palatable to all only through the propaganda of the cook."


    (Leopold Kohr, The Breakdown of Nations)

  4. New technologies

    In order to mold this new decentralized stateless society, we must all familiarize ourselves with the technologies that will support it. This means you, the reader, have a personal responsibility to collect information on, understand and discuss these technologies. You can start by searching the internet or talking to people you deem knowledgeable on new technologies. These are some topics you can research: decentralization, cryptography, cryptocurrency, blockchain, Free software, non-fungible token, decentralized finance, the onion router, IPFS.

  5. Identity

    Your identity is currently verified by your state. Without a state, how will you prove your identity? New digital identity systems will emerge. However, you must not trust your state to handle the identification process. How can you tell whether a digital identification system is trustworthy? Decentralized ID systems will also emerge: your identity will live on a blockchain and be verified not only by the network, but also by people in your social group who can vouch for the accordance of the information to your real person.

  6. Laws

    States control the laws that govern our lives. Without a centralized state and its political system, who will write and approve our laws? Fair, decentralized systems need to be engineered to handle legislature: the people of a certain state or region might, for example, decide to digitalize their laws and implement a system that allows everybody to use their digital decentralized identity to vote on proposals that other citizens have made; and allow anybody that meets certain requirements to make proposals within a certain limit. Workers and consumers of a certain industry might decide to implement a similar system to handle its regulation.

  7. Private finance

    A multitude of decentralized currencies have emerged in the past years. States are now also developing digital currencies, centralized ones. You must not trust these, as they only reinforce the states’ authority and allow an unacceptable amount of surveillance. You should only use decentralized currencies, and prefer ones that don’t require a lot of energy to be created.

  8. Taxation and public finance

    Once society is completely detached from the state, taxation will change radically, as no central body remains to demand it. To reach this point, communities will have to arrange systems to provide funds to bodies now heavily dependent on taxes, such as education, social security, health, and defense, so that they can continue to serve society independently of centralized national governments. For instance, a local community could decide to institute a common fund, persistent or temporary, for a more or less specific purpose, and have all participants contribute a small amount, once or periodically. If said fund is generic in purpose, money will be allocated to projects according to the people's proposals. Systems like these must be engineered with transparency and integrity always in mind, and they must be reviewed by a large number of engineers. None of these diverse solutions for public funding schemes developed by communities will be perfect, but they will gradually evolve as we observe the effects, share solutions and learn from each other.

  9. Environment, waste and energy

    The global climate has been altered by human activity. We must all be conscious that the environment needs protection, and can be changed for the better. Arid regions need reforestation, we must reduce our use of oil, plastic, beef and pork and substitute them for clean or nuclear energy, recyclable or biodegradable materials, and other sources of proteins: plants, chicken, eggs, artificial meat, and insects.

  10. Transition

    The transition to this stateless society begins here and now, with you. Start thinking about how our current institutions can be replaced by decentralized systems that are safe and fair. As local communities develop and adopt successful systems, communities elsewhere will start to emulate them, and contribute to the improvement of said systems.
